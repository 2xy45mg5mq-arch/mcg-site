THE PREMISE

Nothing Is Free

After a hostile takeover, an HR manager makes a dangerous deal to save her staffonly to discover they're more replaceable than she thought.

HUMAN RESOURCE is a corporate thriller with horror elementsnot horror-first. Think Severance meets Margin Call: the dread of institutional complicity, the dark comedy of corporate jargon, and a protagonist who discovers she's been feeding the monster all along.

THE STRUCTURE

Contained Monster in the House

The script follows a contained single-location structurethe office building becomes both haven and prison. This isn't just a budget consideration. It's thematic.

The four-act structure mirrors a fiscal year: Q1 (Lean In), Q2 (Lean Operations), Q3 (Going Remote), Q4 (Liquidation). Each "quarter" escalates the horror while Shae descends deeper into complicity.

"The office is a character. It's where people give their lives thinking they're building something. Then they discover what they've actually been building."

The "monster in the house" trope gets inverted: the monster isn't an intruder. The house is the monster. And Shae has been sleeping in the meditation room for years.

THE WRITER'S VANTAGE

550 Drafts Over Three Years

I spent years working in hedge funds and tech companies before writing HUMAN RESOURCE. The details aren't speculationthey're documentation.

The script emerged from watching colleagues get displaced by automation, seeing "restructuring specialists" hollow out companies, and recognizing the precise corporate language that makes all of it feel inevitable. Normal. Even beneficial.

"You are rented labor accepting validation, frivolity, and a false sense of security in lieu of material compensation."

The horror isn't the machine in the basement. It's that everyone saw it coming. They just called it "optimization."

THE THEMES

Corporate Dehumanization Made Literal

Corporations that treat workers as "human capital" and "resources" taken to their logical extremeactual harvesting of human bodies for profit.

But this isn't torture porn. The horror is in the complicity cascade: watching Shae go from company true believer to enabler to reluctant hero. She doesn't wake up as a monster. She builds herself into one, promotion by promotion, compromise by compromise.

"The failure of 'lean in' feminism: Shae represents women who believe working harder and sacrificing everything will earn them success. Ellsie represents the system that exploits this belief."

THE PROOF OF CONCEPT

From Short to Feature

The proof-of-concept short filmstarring Veanne Cox (You've Got Mail, Erin Brockovich) and Mia Vallet (Hightown, Blindspot)was shot at an actual AI company office. Not a set. A real tech startup that let us film in their space.

That's the vantage point this project brings: not speculating about corporate horror, but documenting it from the inside.

The short has screened at Chattanooga Film Festival, Screamfest NOLA, Malibu Film Festival, and Downtown Film Festival NYC. It's validation that the concept worksand that audiences are hungry for workplace horror that feels real.
